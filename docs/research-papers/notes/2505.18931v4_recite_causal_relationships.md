# ReCITE: Eliciting Causal Relationships from Large Language Models — Notes

**arXiv:** 2505.18931v4 | **Authors:** Saklad, Chadha, Pavlov, Moraffah (WPI + Apple)
**Title:** Can Large Language Models Infer Causal Relationships from Real-World Text?

## Overview

Introduces **ReCITE** (Real-world CausalIty from Textual Evidence), the first benchmark for evaluating LLM causal reasoning on real-world academic texts. Previous benchmarks used synthetic or simplified texts with explicit causal statements. ReCITE uses 292 real academic papers containing causal loop diagrams, requiring models to reconstruct complex causal graphs (5-140 nodes, 6-205 edges) from lengthy documents where causal relationships are often implicit.

## Benchmark Construction

**Source:** 646 papers searched via "causal loop diagram" keyword from open-access repositories (MDPI, PLOS). Filtered to 292 papers with a single, clear causal graph.

**Data characteristics:**
- Text length: 684 to 171,141 characters (mean 40,541)
- 17 distinct fields: Environmental Science (20.2%), Engineering (20.2%), Business/Management (12.0%), only 5.5% Economics
- 92.9% of 6,378 unique causal concepts appear in only one paper — extreme semantic diversity
- 87.7% of causal events mentioned in text (explicitness), but many are implicit rather than explicit
- 90.4% of graphs contain feedback cycles

**Annotation pipeline:** Human annotators transcribed causal graphs to "source -> sink" format. Double-annotated 13% of samples: edge-level F1=0.984, Cohen's kappa=0.987.

**Text processing:** PyMuPDF extraction -> Mistral Small for markdown conversion (removing figures, references, appendices) -> o3-mini to remove explicit graph references and fix inconsistencies.

## Evaluation Framework

**LLM-as-a-Judge** (DeepSeek v3.2) evaluates at node and edge levels, comparing generated elements against both ground-truth graph AND source text. Three assessment dimensions per element:
1. **Presence** — strong match, weak match, or no match
2. **Semantic similarity** — strong, moderate, weak
3. **Abstraction level** — broader, aligned, or narrower than reference

Recall is weighted by element **importance** (core, intermediate, peripheral). SHD computed from judge's edge-level evaluations.

Cross-judge validation: four candidate judges show strong pairwise correlation (r=0.78-0.91), no family bias detected.

## Key Results

### Overall Performance (Table 3)

Best model (Claude Opus 4.5) achieves only **F1=0.535**. All models show high precision but low recall — they generate plausible causal relationships but fail to recover the specific ground-truth structure.

|-------|--------|--------|--------|--------|-----|----------|
| Claude Opus 4.5 | 0.972 | 0.571 | 0.853 | 0.305 | **0.535** | 0.108 |
| GPT 5.2 | 0.960 | 0.524 | 0.804 | 0.309 | 0.510 | 0.130 |
| Gemini 3 Pro | 0.980 | 0.527 | 0.865 | 0.281 | 0.501 | 0.114 |
| DeepSeek R1 | 0.955 | 0.504 | 0.773 | 0.231 | 0.460 | 0.122 |
| Qwen 2.5 32B | 0.925 | 0.431 | 0.686 | 0.180 | 0.386 | 0.107 |
| Llama 3.1 8B | 0.916 | 0.341 | 0.615 | 0.111 | 0.295 | 0.103 |

Reasoning models outperform instruction-tuned. Positive correlation between model size and performance. Direction reversals are rare (<1.1% of matched edges) — when models find relationships, they get the direction right.

### Node Identification vs. Causal Reasoning (Table 4)

Providing ground-truth node names barely helped — F1 improved by only +0.037 to +0.042 for strong models, and actually decreased for weaker models. **The bottleneck is causal reasoning, not entity recognition.**

### Effect of Explicitness

Strong positive correlation between explicitness and performance. F1 drops by ~50% between highest and lowest explicitness bins. Even at highest explicitness, all models perform poorly. This is the single most important difficulty factor.

### Effect of Size

Counterintuitively, weak **positive** correlation between text/graph size and performance. This is confounded by explicitness — larger texts tend to be more explicit (R²=0.171 for text length). Graph density and cyclicity have no effect on performance.

### Effect of Domain

No statistically significant domain effect (ANOVA F=1.03, p=0.38). Performance gap between best and worst clusters is only 0.041 F1.

### Case Study (R1 on Qinba Mountains livelihood paper)

Even with explicit textual descriptions ("sunshine, precipitation, and arable land area were selected to represent the natural capital"), R1 recovered only 1 of 4 ground-truth nodes, missed all 3 ground-truth edges, and added 2 spurious nodes with 2 spurious edges. The model identified relevant concepts during reasoning but failed to integrate them into the correct graph structure.

## Relevance to DM-Align Project

1. **LLM causal reasoning is fundamentally weak.** Even Claude Opus 4.5 scores 0.535 F1 on reconstructing causal graphs from real texts. This contextualizes our Corr2Cause improvement (+38pp) — our model improved on formal causal inference questions, but that's multiple-choice, not graph construction. The gap between recognizing causal relationships in structured formats vs. inferring them from text is enormous.

2. **Explicitness matters more than anything.** Our training data uses explicit DM causal reasoning (cause A leads to effect B through mechanism C). The paper shows models trained on explicit causal text will struggle when causal relationships are implicit. This explains why our model regresses on EconCausal — economic texts often state causal relationships implicitly, and our model hasn't learned to extract implicit causality.

3. **The precision-recall gap is telling.** Models generate many plausible but incorrect causal relationships (high precision, low recall). They're confident but wrong about the specific structure. This maps to our finding that the finetuned model hedges too much — it generates skeptical responses that sound reasonable but miss the actual causal direction.

4. **Direction reversals are rare.** When LLMs identify causal relationships, they almost never flip the direction. This suggests the problem is about identifying WHICH relationships exist, not about understanding causal direction. For our project, this means the model's causal reasoning isn't producing inverted causality — it's producing the wrong relationships entirely.

5. **Benchmark cost is low.** All experiments under $1,000. ReCITE could be adapted as an evaluation benchmark for our DM-aligned model's causal reasoning capabilities, using DM-themed causal loop diagrams.

## Limitations

- English-only, academic literature only
- Models given target number of nodes (simplifies task)
- Zero-shot only, no few-shot or RAG
- LLM-as-a-Judge evaluation, though validated
- Drawn from system dynamics causal loop diagrams specifically, may not generalize to other causal graph paradigms
