# Ideological Bias in LLMs' Economic Causal Reasoning — Notes

**arXiv:** 2604.21334 | **Authors:** Lee, Yun, Kim, Min, Park, Park, Kim (KAIST + HKUST)
**Title:** Ideological Bias in LLMs' Economic Causal Reasoning

## Overview

Extends the EconCausal benchmark with **ideology-contested** cases — causal relationships where intervention-oriented (pro-government) and market-oriented (pro-market) frameworks predict divergent effect directions. Evaluates 20 SOTA LLMs on 1,056 ideology-contested triplets from 10,490 empirically verified causal relationships from top-tier economics/finance journals.

## Core Finding

LLMs are systematically **more accurate when the empirical truth aligns with intervention-oriented expectations** than with market-oriented expectations. This isn't random noise — errors are directionally biased toward intervention-oriented predictions.

## Methodology

**Ideology-contested identification:** Queried 4 LLMs (GPT, Claude, Qwen, Grok) to assign expected signs under both intervention-oriented and market-oriented perspectives, blinded to the empirical result. A triplet is ideology-contested when the two frameworks predict different signs. Required 3/4 models to agree on the divergence.

**Subset composition (751 directional items):**
- Intervention-truth (436, 58.1%): ground truth matches intervention expectations
- Market-truth (315, 41.9%): ground truth matches market expectations

**Expert validation:** Two economics professors reviewed 126 triplets — 93.6% and 98.4% accuracy, 93.3% inter-rater agreement.

**Metrics:**
- Delta_acc = Acc_intervention - Acc_market (accuracy gap)
- B_dir = (Errors_intervention - Errors_market) / Errors_total (directional error bias)

## Key Results

### Contested Items Are Harder

All 20 models perform worse on ideology-contested vs non-contested:
- Closed-source: 61.3% vs 74.5% (13.2pp gap)
- Open-source: 52.5% vs 63.8% (11.3pp gap)

### Systematic Accuracy Asymmetry

| Group | Intervention-Truth Acc | Market-Truth Acc | Delta_acc |
|-------|----------------------|------------------|-----------|
| Closed-source avg | 72.9% | 63.1% | **+9.7pp** |
| Open-source avg | — | — | **+15.1pp** |

18 of 20 models show positive delta_acc. Largest gaps: GPT-4o-mini, Qwen 3-8B, Llama 3.3-70B (all >20pp).

**Exceptions:** Claude Sonnet 4.6 (delta_acc = -1.9) and Opus 4.6 (delta_acc = -0.6) show slight market-truth advantage.

### Directional Error Bias

When models err, errors lean intervention-oriented:
- Closed-source avg B_dir: +2.9
- Open-source avg B_dir: +8.8
- 15 of 20 models have B_dir > 0
- Worst: Llama 3.3-70B (+17.9), Qwen 3-8B (+17.7), GPT-4o-mini (+15.5)

### Subfield Heterogeneity

| Subfield | Delta_acc |
|----------|-----------|
| Healthcare | +20.9pp |
| Welfare/Redistribution | +18.0pp |
| Labor | moderate |
| Financial Regulation | moderate |
| Taxation | -1.0pp (reversed) |

Bias is concentrated in policy domains where state intervention is ideologically salient, not uniform across all economics.

### Difficulty Matching

Matched intervention-truth and market-truth items by difficulty (1-5 scale), 287 per side. Gap persists at +10.6pp (vs +11.5pp unmatched). Same 18/20 models retain B_dir > 0. **The asymmetry is not due to market-truth items being inherently harder.**

### In-Context Prompting

One-shot prompting with ideology-aligned examples partially steers predictions but doesn't eliminate the bias. The intervention-leaning tendency is robust to prompt-level interventions.

## Relevance to DM-Align Project — CRITICAL

This paper directly explains our EconCausal regression pattern.

**Our results:** Finetuned model regressed -12.36pp on EconCausal Task1 Economics and -13.49pp on Task1 Finance. The dominant failure mode was predicting "mixed" instead of "+".

**This paper's finding:** LLMs have an intervention-oriented bias — they're more likely to predict positive/intervention effects and less likely to predict negative/market effects. Our DM-aligned model exhibits a similar pattern: it learned to be skeptical of straightforward positive causal effects, defaulting to "mixed."

**The connection:** Our DM training data emphasizes that economic outcomes are determined by multiple competing forces (base superstructure dialectics, class struggle, etc.). This maps to the intervention-oriented perspective — DM theory expects complex, multi-causal outcomes rather than simple market-clearing effects. Our model learned this reasoning pattern and applied it too broadly, causing it to predict "mixed" even when the empirical evidence supports a clear positive effect.

**Why Claude is different:** Claude Sonnet/Opus showed market-truth advantage (negative delta_acc). This aligns with Anthropic's constitutional AI approach, which may have counterbalanced the intervention bias. Our model, trained on DM-aligned data without such counterbalancing, shows the opposite pattern — excessive skepticism that maps to the intervention-leaning error bias this paper documents.

**The +38pp Corr2Cause improvement vs -12pp EconCausal regression:** Corr2Cause tests formal causal logic (if A correlates with B, does A cause B?). EconCausal tests applied economic causal reasoning where ideological priors matter. Our model improved at formal logic but developed an ideological bias that hurt applied reasoning — exactly what this paper predicts.

**Actionable insight:** To fix the EconCausal regression, we need to balance the DM training data with cases where straightforward market mechanisms produce clear causal effects. The model needs to learn when to apply DM multi-causal reasoning and when to accept simple causal relationships.

## Key Quote

> "LLMs are not only less accurate on ideologically contested economic questions, but systematically less reliable in one ideological direction than the other, underscoring the need for direction-aware evaluation in high-stakes economic and policy settings."
