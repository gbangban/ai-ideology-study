## CLAIM 1: Global Average Carbon Price is $3-4 per Tonne

**Claim:** "As of 2026, the global average price is only about $3 to $4 per tonne."

**VERDICT: PARTIALLY ACCURATE - depends on which metric you use.**

### What the sources actually say:

- **IMF (2021):** States "the global average price is still only $3 per ton" (Parry, Black, Roaf 2021, p.5). This is the global emissions-weighted average including unpriced emissions at $0.

- **World Bank State and Trends 2025:** Reports TWO different metrics:
  - Average carbon price for **implemented instruments only**: ~$19/tCO2e in 2025 (up from ~$10 in 2015)
  - Global emissions-weighted average (including unpriced emissions at $0): ~$5/tCO2e in 2025

- **World Bank State and Trends 2026 (May 2026 press release):** "The average carbon price is now nearly $21/tCO2e" - this is the average across implemented instruments only.

### Assessment:
The $3-$5 figure is correct for the IMF's methodology (total global revenue / all global emissions including unpriced). The claim says "$3 to $4" which matches IMF's 2021 figure of $3, but the World Bank's 2025 report shows this has risen to ~$5 globally. The claim is slightly outdated (reflecting 2021 data) but directionally correct. The $21 figure from the World Bank 2026 report is the average across only the ~30% of emissions that are priced, which is a fundamentally different metric.

## CLAIM 2: High-Level Commission Recommends $75-100 "Immediately"

**Claim:** "A global price of at least $75 to $100 USD per tonne of CO2 is needed immediately."

**VERDICT: INACCURATE - misrepresents the HLCCP timeline.**

### What the sources actually say:

- **High-Level Commission on Carbon Prices (2017, Stiglitz & Stern):** Concluded that carbon price should be $40-$80/tCO2 by 2020 and $50-$100/tCO2 by 2030. This was for 2°C scenarios, not 1.5°C.

- **IMF (2021 Parry et al.):** States "additional measures equivalent to a global carbon tax of around $75 per ton by 2030 (and rising further beyond 2030)." This is a 2030 target, not an "immediate" requirement.

- **IMF (2022 Getting on Track to Net Zero):** "The global average carbon price consistent with the 2°C scenarios is around $80 per tonne, while that for the 1.8°C scenario is $100 per tonne." These are 2030 figures.

### Assessment:
The claim incorrectly frames a 2030 target as an "immediate" requirement. The $75 figure comes from the IMF's 2030 target for high-income countries under the ICPF proposal. The HLCCP's $50-$100 range is a 2030 target, not a current one. This is a significant distortion.

## CLAIM 3: 2030 Target of $120-150 per Tonne

**Claim:** "The OECD and IPCC IAMs project that this global average must hit roughly $120 to $150 USD per tonne by 2030."

**VERDICT: PARTIALLY ACCURATE - within IAM range but represents upper end.**

### What the sources actually say:

- **IPCC SR15 (Figure 2.26):** For 1.5°C pathways (SSP1-19, SSP2-19, SSP5-19), carbon prices in 2030 range from $0 to $600+ per tonne, with median values around $100-$200. For 2°C pathways (SSP1-26, SSP2-26, SSP5-26), the 2030 range is $0-$150, with median around $50-$100.

- **Guivarch & Rogelj (IIASA, RCP2.6 analysis):** Carbon prices in RCP2.6 scenarios range from $15 to $360/tCO2e in 2030 (in 2005 USD). The median is much lower than $120-$150.

- **IMF (2022):** For 2°C scenarios, the global average carbon price is ~$80/tonne, with HICs at $115, MICs at $65, and LICs at $45.

- **HLCCP (2017):** $50-$100/tCO2 by 2030 for 2°C.

### Assessment:
$120-$150 by 2030 is within the IAM range but represents the upper quartile for 2°C scenarios. It's more consistent with 1.5°C pathways. The claim attributes this specifically to "OECD and IPCC IAMs" but neither institution has published this specific consensus range. The IPCC IAM ensemble shows a much wider range ($15-$360), and the IMF's more authoritative figure for 2°C is ~$80 globally ($115 for HICs). The claim is not wrong per se, but it cherry-picks the higher end of the range and presents it as a consensus.

## CLAIM 4: 2040-2050 Price of $200-400+ per Tonne

**Claim:** "To incentivize net-negative emissions technologies later in the century, the implicit international price must scale to $200-$400+ USD per tonne."

**VERDICT: ACCURATE - consistent with IAM literature.**

### What the sources actually say:

- **Guivarch & Rogelj (IIASA):** RCP2.6 scenarios show prices of $45-$1,000/tCO2e in 2050 (in 2005 USD). The $200-$400 range falls well within this.

- **Bednar et al. (Nature, 2021):** Discusses carbon prices exceeding $400-$500/tCO2 by 2050 for net-negative emissions scenarios.

- **LGIM Net Zero 2050 Report:** Below 2°C scenario requires $205/tCO2e by 2050; Net Zero 1.5°C requires $491/tCO2e by 2050.

- **NGFS Technical Documentation:** Shows IAM carbon prices reaching $400-$800+ by 2050-2060 in Net Zero 2050 scenarios.

### Assessment:
The $200-$400+ range for 2040-2050 is well-supported by the literature. It's on the lower end for 1.5°C pathways and the middle of the range for 2°C pathways.

## CLAIM 5: IMF Tiered ICPF ($75/$50/$25)

**Claim:** High-income $75, Middle-income $50, Low-income $25 per tonne by 2030.

**VERDICT: ACCURATE - directly from IMF source documents.**

### What the sources say:

- **IMF Staff Climate Notes 2021/001 (p.11-12):** "Scenarios are considered where advanced/EME countries are subject to price floors of $50 per ton each, or $75, $50, and $25 for advanced, high-income EME (for example, China), and low-income EME (for example, India) countries, respectively."

- **IMF 2022 Getting on Track to Net Zero:** "International carbon price floor (ICPF) 2°C: All HIC, MIC, and LIC countries implement a minimum carbon price of $75, $50, and $25 per tonne in 2030, respectively."

- **PWC/WEF ICPF Analysis:** Confirms identical figures.

- **Banque de France:** "The ICPF is a proposal to introduce carbon price floors... with the floors set at USD 25 tCO2eq for low-income countries, USD 50 tCO2eq for middle-income countries and USD 75 tCO2eq for high-income countries."

### Important nuance the claim misses:
- All figures are in **2018 USD** (IMF footnote 28)
- The IMF explicitly states these are **floor prices**, not target prices - countries may need to set higher prices to meet their NDCs
- The IMF model shows these floors would achieve only the upper end of the 2°C range, not 1.5°C
- India's emissions would actually RISE in absolute terms under the $25 floor (by 21%), reflecting differentiated responsibilities

## CLAIM 6: ~30% Emissions Covered, ~70% Unpriced

**Claim:** "Nearly 30% of global greenhouse gas emissions are covered by some form of carbon pricing."

**VERDICT: ACCURATE - matches World Bank 2026 data.**

### What the sources say:

- **World Bank 2026 (May 2026):** "Nearly 30% of global greenhouse gas emissions are covered by a direct carbon price across 87 implemented policies." More precisely: "Just over 29% of global greenhouse gas emissions worldwide are now covered by direct carbon pricing."

- **World Bank 2025:** "Around 28% of global greenhouse gas emissions are now covered by a carbon price."

- **IMF 2021:** "Over 60 pricing schemes now implemented" (at the time of writing)

### Assessment:
The 30% figure is accurate for 2026. The claim correctly attributes this to the World Bank.

## CLAIM 8: Revenue-Weighted Average Calculation ($2.01/tonne)

**Claim:** "$107 billion / 53 billion tonnes = $2.01 USD per tonne."

**VERDICT: MATHEMATICALLY CORRECT but MISLEADING comparison.**

### Analysis:

The math is correct: $107B / 53B tonnes = $2.02/tonne (the claim says $2.01, off by a cent).

However, this calculation is misleading because:
1. The $107B revenue covers only ~29% of emissions that face a direct carbon price
2. The remaining ~71% of emissions face $0 direct price but may face indirect pricing through regulations, subsidies, and other measures
3. The World Bank's own global emissions-weighted average of ~$5/tonne accounts for the fact that some unpriced emissions face implicit carbon costs through fuel taxes and other instruments
4. The $2.01 figure treats all unpriced emissions as having exactly $0 cost, which ignores existing fuel taxes, energy taxes, and regulatory costs that effectively price carbon

The IMF's own methodology is more sophisticated than simple revenue/emissions division - it accounts for effective carbon rates including pre-existing fuel taxes.

## CLAIM 9: EU ETS Trading at EUR 80-100/tonne

**Claim:** "European Union ETS (trading around EUR 80-EUR 100/tonne)"

**VERDICT: ACCURATE for current prices, but overstated as a range.**

### What the data shows:

- **Current (June 2026):** EU carbon allowances settled at EUR 78.22/tCO2 on June 4, 2026. Trading Economics shows EUR 80.66 on June 17, 2026.

- **2025 average:** EUR 73.43 (auction) / EUR 74.35 (secondary market)

- **All-time high:** EUR 105.73 (February 2023)

- **CBAM certificate price Q1 2026:** EUR 75.36/tCO2e

### Assessment:
The current price is ~EUR 75-81, which is at the low end of the claimed EUR 80-100 range. EUR 100 has not been sustained since mid-2023. The claim's range is slightly optimistic but not wrong - prices have traded in this range at various points.

## CLAIM 10: Canada Carbon Price Path Over $130/tonne

**Claim:** "High-income countries like Canada have established domestic paths aiming for over $130/tonne."

**VERDICT: PARTIALLY ACCURATE - but significantly complicated by recent changes.**

### What the sources say:

- **Original federal benchmark (2021):** Canada's carbon price was supposed to reach $170/tonne by 2030.

- **Updated trajectory (May 2026):** The federal government revised the headline price trajectory:
  - $95 in 2026
  - $100 in 2027-2029
  - $115 in 2030
  - $130 in 2035
  - $140 in 2040

- **CBC News (May 2026):** "Canada's headline carbon price was supposed to rise to $170/tonne by 2030" but the new trajectory delays $130/tonne to 2035 (previously targeted for 2030 under the original plan).

- **Alberta deal (May 2026):** Federal and Alberta governments agreed on $130/tonne by 2040 for industrial carbon pricing.

### Assessment:
The claim that Canada aims for "over $130/tonne" is technically true ($130 by 2035, $140 by 2040), but the original, more ambitious target of $170 by 2030 has been scaled back. The claim doesn't mention this significant reduction in ambition, which is a material omission.

## CLAIM 11: CBAM Mechanism Description

**Claim:** CBAM forces lower-pricing nations to raise domestic carbon prices to keep revenue rather than forfeit it as tariff.

**VERDICT: ACCURATE - correct description of CBAM economic logic.**

### What the sources confirm:

- **EU CBAM Regulation (EU) 2023/956:** CBAM certificates are priced at the average EU ETS auction clearing price. Importers must surrender certificates covering embedded emissions.

- **Q1 2026 CBAM certificate price:** EUR 75.36/tCO2e - the first official CBAM certificate price in history.

- **2026 phase-in:** Only 2.5% of embedded emissions require certificate coverage in 2026, phasing up to 100% by 2034.

- **IMF 2021:** "In the absence of internationally coordinated carbon pricing, jurisdictions moving ahead with aggressive carbon pricing may well impose BCAs... BCAs are far less efficient and effective than price floors."

### Assessment:
The economic logic is correct - if a exporting country has a carbon price, the exporter pays domestically and doesn't pay the CBAM tariff. If they don't, the importer pays the CBAM tariff to the EU. This creates an incentive for exporting countries to implement their own carbon pricing.

## CLAIM 12: World Bank vs IMF Methodology Discrepanancy Explanation

**Claim:** "The IMF averages the price across all global emissions (including unpriced ones), while the World Bank typically reports the average price strictly within active markets."

**VERDICT: MOSTLY ACCURATE but oversimplified.**

### What the sources show:

The World Bank actually reports BOTH metrics:
1. Average carbon price for implemented instruments (~$21/tCO2e in 2026)
2. Global emissions-weighted average including unpriced emissions (~$5/tCO2e in 2025)

The IMF also uses a more sophisticated "effective carbon rate" methodology that includes pre-existing fuel taxes converted to CO2-equivalent rates, not just explicit carbon pricing revenue.

The claim correctly identifies the core difference in approach but oversimplifies by suggesting the World Bank only reports one metric.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The most significant distortion** is framing the IMF's 2030 target ($75/tonne for HICs) as an "immediate" requirement. This is a 2030 goal, not a current baseline.

2. **The $120-150/tonne 2030 figure** is not a consensus number. It represents the upper quartile of IAM results for 2°C scenarios. The IMF's own estimate for a global 2°C-compatible price is ~$80/tonne.

3. **The tiered ICPF ($75/$50/$25) is accurately reported** and directly sourced from IMF documents. However, the claim doesn't note these are in 2018 dollars.

4. **Coverage and revenue figures are accurate** and match the latest World Bank reports.

5. **The revenue-weighted average calculation** ($2.01/tonne) is mathematically correct but ignores effective carbon rates from fuel taxes and regulations that the IMF accounts for in its own methodology.

6. **Canada's carbon price trajectory** has been significantly reduced from the original $170/tonne by 2030 target to $130 by 2035, which the claim doesn't reflect.
